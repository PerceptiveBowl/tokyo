Tokyo

Capital and Largest City: Tokyo serves as the capital and largest city of Japan. Situated on the island of Honshu, in the Kanto region, on the southeastern side of the main island.
Population The city proper has a population of over 14 million, making it one of the most populous urban areas in the world.
Historical Name: Tokyo was formerly known as Edo, the name of the small fishing village that existed on the site for centuries.
Capital Shift: The capital of Japan was moved to Edo (renamed Tokyo) after the Meiji Restoration in 1868.
Tax-Free Shopping: Tokyo is a great place for tax-free shopping, particularly for tourists.
Tokyo's Oldest Temple: Tokyo's oldest temple is possibly Sensō-ji in Asakusa, founded in 628.
The Ainu
The Ainu are believed to be descendants of an indigenous population that once spread across northern Asia, and they are considered to be the earliest settlers of Hokkaido, Japan's northern island.
Basic Location
Tokyo is the capital city of Japan and it is located in the region of Kantō. It is also located in the larest Island of Japan named Honshu which lies in the ring of fire.
Bordering Prefectures
Bording Prefectures are nearby districts of Tokyo which are Saitama located on the North of Tokyo. Chiba located on the East, Kanagawa located on the South and Yamanashi on the West of Tokyo
Exact Location
Tokyo is primarily located on the southeastern side of Honshu, encompassing the mainland and also the island chains of Izu and Ogasawara island. The cordinates are of Tokyo are: Latitude: 35.6895° N Longitude: 139.6917° E
Culture
The city’s ethnic group is homogenous, with 97.9% of the population having Japanese as their ethnicity. However, there are smaller ethnic groups like Korean, Brazilian, Chinese, Vietnamese and Filipino living there. The majority of the people here believe in Buddhism and Shinto, with Christianity following when it comes to their faith/religion.
Native Culture
In terms of indigenous people, Tokyo is primarily made up of the Yamato people, who are also the majority in the nation. Whilst it does not have its own native people, the Ainu, Ryūkyūans and Ōbeikei people have recently migrated (19 - 20th century) from their original native land to Tokyo.

Push and Pull Factors

Push

1.

Limited Opportunities in Rural Areas: Many rural areas in Japan face declining populations and limited economic opportunities, prompting people to migrate to Tokyo in search of better prospects.

2.

Aging Population and Social Services: Tokyo boasts numerous prestigious universities and specialized training programs, drawing students from across Japan and internationally.

3.

Natural Disasters: Japan, including rural areas, is prone to natural disasters like earthquakes, tsunamis, and typhoons. The impact of these disasters on rural communities, coupled with insufficient recovery infrastructure, encouraged many people to migrate to Tokyo where infrastructure is more resilient.

4.

Modernization of Agriculture: The mechanization and automation of agriculture in rural Japan have decreased the demand for farm labor. This push for fewer agricultural jobs led many people to leave rural areas and move to cities like Tokyo, where there are more industrial and service-sector jobs.

5.

Educational Limitations: Many rural areas lack advanced educational institutions, including universities and research centers. Students who seek higher education are drawn to Tokyo for its prestigious universities like the University of Tokyo and other higher education opportunities.

Pull

1.

Job Opportunities: Tokyo is a major economic hub, offering a wide array of job opportunities, especially in finance, technology, and creative industries. This attracts people seeking better career prospects.

2.

Tokyo boasts numerous prestigious universities and specialized training programs, drawing students from across Japan and internationally.

3.

Cultural and Entertainment Amenities: Tokyo is renowned for its vibrant culture, diverse entertainment options, world-class dining, and unique shopping experiences, making it an appealing place to live.

4.

Educational Institutions: As Japan’s education hub, Tokyo hosts some of the best universities in Asia, such as the University of Tokyo. These institutions attract students and academics from across Japan and internationally who are drawn to Tokyo’s cutting-edge research and academic opportunities.

5.

Diverse Population: Tokyo is one of the most cosmopolitan cities in the world, with people from all over Japan and the world calling it home. This diversity fosters a sense of inclusivity and draws people looking for a multicultural experience, which is not always available in rural areas.

Tokyo Population Trends

Age population

When it comes to the age ratios, people from 65 and over make up 33% of the population, but in the next upcoming years, this will substantially decline. In turns of working ages (20-64), this will also drop to the 4.8 - 5.6 million range (2020 - 2030), which is less then the 7.7 - 8.3 million range (2010 - 2020). This is due to the lack of younger residents, couples no longer having children or facing fertility problems and the increase in elderly care. In terms of gender, the men to women ratio is very similar, with women making up 50.1 - 51% of the population and men making up 49 - 49.9%. For every 100 women, there is 96 men.

Negatives

Congestion Pollution

“Heat Island” Effect: The dense urban congestion and environment and human activities generate waste heat, which makes the island heat up and make it harder to cool down, increasing the temperature, and possible health problems.

Air Pollution: Industrialization and increased traffic contribute to air pollution, impacting residents' health and the environment. This pollution consists of nitrofgen oxides and other harmful pollutants, which pose serious health risks, like asthma and other respiratory or cardiovascular diseases and issues.

Negatives

Carbon Emissions:

Increased energy consumption and transportation contribute to higher carbon emissions, exacerbating climate change.

Traffic Congestion:

Increased traffic due to a larger population can lead to chronic congestion and commute problems.

Resource strains:

Due to ithe ncreasing population, there is a much higher demand in resources, including food, water and essentials like clothes or homes. This leads to fewer resources for the population, leading to poverty and more problems.

HOW TO IMPROVE SUSTAINABILITY IN TOKYO


Reducing CO2 Emmissions and Being Energy Efficient: There are several ways Tokyo’s sustainability can be improved, which include:

Promoting renewable energy - Promoting renewable energy can help persuade people from switching to switch from coal and fossil fuel to energy sources such as solar, wind or biomass.
Reducing emissions - Tokyo can reduce emissions through techniques like using zero-emission vehicles (electric), using renewable energy sources and implementing energy management strategies.
Improving public transport - Using vehicles that do not rely on petrol or fossil fuels but rather using either human-powered vehicles (scooters, bikes…) or electric vehicles (buses, trains…).
Engaging the community to eco-friendly practices - Implementing practices like recycling, reusing materials, using eco-friendly packing or bags for shopping and promoting the reduction of food waste through composting are multiple ways that can be used to engage the community in eco-friendly practices and help improve sustainability.
